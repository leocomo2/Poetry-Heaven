Poetic Forms Explained

Explore different poetic structures and their unique characteristics.

Sonnet

+

A 14-line poem with a specific rhyme scheme, traditionally exploring themes of love. The two most common forms are the Petrarchan (Italian) and Shakespearean (English).

Example: Shakespearean Sonnet

14 lines, rhyme scheme: ABAB CDCD EFEF GG
Three quatrains and a final rhyming couplet
Typically written in iambic pentameter

Learn More

Haiku

+

A traditional Japanese form consisting of three lines with a 5-7-5 syllable structure, often capturing a moment in nature.

Example Structure

First line: 5 syllables
Second line: 7 syllables
Third line: 5 syllables

Learn More

Villanelle

+

A 19-line poem with five tercets followed by a quatrain, using a specific pattern of repetition.

Key Features

Rhyme scheme: ABA ABA ABA ABA ABA ABAA
Lines 1 and 3 repeat alternately in subsequent stanzas
Both lines appear together in the final stanza

Learn More

Free Verse

+

Poetry that doesn't follow regular meter or rhyme schemes, relying on other elements for rhythm and structure.

Characteristics

No set rhyme scheme or meter
Relies on natural speech rhythms
May use other poetic devices like imagery and metaphor

Poetry Glossary

Look up poetic terms and devices to deepen your understanding.

Metaphor

A figure of speech that makes a comparison between two unlike things without using "like" or "as".

Example: "All the world's a stage" (Shakespeare)

Enjambment

The continuation of a sentence or clause across a line break without punctuation.

Example: "I think that I shall never see / A poem lovely as a tree"

Iambic Pentameter

A metrical pattern in poetry consisting of five iambs (unstressed syllable followed by a stressed syllable) per line.

Example: "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?"

Alliteration

The repetition of consonant sounds at the beginning of words close to each other.

Example: "Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers"

Volta

The turn in thought in a sonnet, often occurring between the octave and sestet in Petrarchan sonnets.

Example: The "But" in line 9 of many sonnets signals the volta.

Synecdoche

A figure of speech in which a part is made to represent the whole or vice versa.

Example: "All hands on deck" (hands represent sailors)
